Reimer-Tiemann Reaction

The Reimer-Tiemann reaction is an organic chemical reaction used primarily for the formylation (introduction of a formyl group, -CHO) of phenols, most notably converting phenol to salicylaldehyde. This reaction is an important method to synthesize aromatic aldehydes.

Key Features of the Reimer-Tiemann Reaction:

  • Reaction Type: The Reimer-Tiemann reaction is a type of electrophilic aromatic substitution.
  • Reactants: The starting material is typically a phenol. The reaction employs chloroform (CHCl₃) and a strong base like sodium hydroxide (NaOH).
  • Products: The main product of the reaction is an aldehyde group (-CHO) ortho to the hydroxyl group (-OH) on the aromatic ring. In the case of phenol, the product is salicylaldehyde.

Reaction Mechanism:

The mechanism begins with the deprotonation of chloroform by sodium hydroxide, forming trichloromethide anion (CCl₃⁻), which rapidly loses a chloride ion to form dichlorocarbene (CCl₂). Dichlorocarbene is the reactive intermediate that attacks the activated ortho or para position of the phenol ring. Hydrolysis of the carbene intermediate then leads to the formation of the aldehyde group (-CHO), producing an aromatic aldehyde.

Mechanism Steps:

  • Generation of dichlorocarbene (CCl₂): Chloroform is treated with a strong base (NaOH), forming the carbene.
  • Electrophilic attack: The carbene attacks the ortho position (or sometimes para) relative to the hydroxyl group of phenol.
  • Rearrangement and hydrolysis: The resulting intermediate undergoes rearrangement and hydrolysis, leading to the formation of an aldehyde.

Example of the Reimer-Tiemann Reaction:

The reaction of phenol with chloroform in the presence of sodium hydroxide yields salicylaldehyde.

Reaction:

C₆H₅OH (phenol) + CHCl₃ (chloroform) + 3 NaOH → C₆H₄(OH)CHO (salicylaldehyde) + NaCl + H₂O

Applications:

  • Synthesis of salicylaldehyde, which is used as a precursor for the synthesis of various pharmaceuticals, dyes, and fragrances.
  • The Reimer-Tiemann reaction is useful for the functionalization of aromatic rings with aldehyde groups, which can further participate in other reactions like condensation or reduction.

Limitations:

  • The reaction works best for phenols but may produce a mixture of ortho and para isomers.
  • It may not work efficiently with electron-withdrawing groups on the aromatic ring, as they deactivate the ring towards electrophilic substitution.
