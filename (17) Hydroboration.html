Hydroboration Reaction

The hydroboration reaction is an important organic reaction used to add hydrogen and boron across a double bond (alkenes) or a triple bond (alkynes). It is widely used in organic synthesis because of its regioselectivity and anti-Markovnikov nature.

General Features:

  • Reagent: The most common reagent used in hydroboration is borane (BH₃), usually stabilized as a complex, such as borane-tetrahydrofuran (BH₃-THF) or diborane (B₂H₆).

Reaction Mechanism:

The reaction involves a concerted process where the boron atom from BH₃ adds to the less substituted carbon of the double bond (anti-Markovnikov addition), while hydrogen adds to the more substituted carbon. This results in the formation of an alkylborane intermediate.

Oxidation Step:

After the initial hydroboration, the alkylborane intermediate can be oxidized using hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) in a basic medium (sodium hydroxide, NaOH). This converts the boron into a hydroxyl group (-OH), giving a primary alcohol.

Key Characteristics:

  • Regioselectivity: The boron atom attaches to the less substituted carbon (anti-Markovnikov), which makes it different from many other reactions (e.g., Markovnikov addition in electrophilic additions).
  • Stereoselectivity: Hydroboration is syn-addition, meaning both the boron and the hydrogen are added to the same side of the double bond, leading to retention of stereochemistry.

Applications:

  • Hydroboration is used to prepare alcohols in a controlled manner.
  • It's useful in the synthesis of organoboranes, which can be further used in various synthetic applications like Suzuki coupling.
